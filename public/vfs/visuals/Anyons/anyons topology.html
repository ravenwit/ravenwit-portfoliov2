Why the Torus forces the vacuum to split

1. The Geometry of Cycles

On a torus $T^2$, the fundamental group is $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$. This means we have two non-contractible loops, the Meridian ($\gamma_x$) and the Longitude ($\gamma_y$). Crucially, these loops must intersect exactly once.

$$ \text{Intersection}(\gamma_x, \gamma_y) = 1 $$

2. The Heisenberg-Weyl Algebra

We define Magnetic Translation Operators $\hat{W}_x$ and $\hat{W}_y$ that move quasiparticles around these loops. Because the loops intersect, the order of operations matters. If we swap the order, the particle worldlines braid around each other, picking up a statistical phase $\theta$.

$$ \hat{W}_y \hat{W}_x \hat{W}_y^{-1} \hat{W}_x^{-1} = e^{i\theta} $$ $$ \implies \hat{W}_y \hat{W}_x = e^{i\theta} \hat{W}_x \hat{W}_y $$

3. Minimal Representation Dimension

If the phase is rational, $\theta = \frac{2\pi p}{k}$, we can prove there is no 1-dimensional representation of this algebra. Thus, the Hilbert space must have at least dimension $k$.

$$ \text{GSD} = k^g $$

Where $g$ is the genus (number of holes). For a Sphere ($g=0$), $\text{GSD} = 1$. For a Torus ($g=1$), $\text{GSD} = k$.
